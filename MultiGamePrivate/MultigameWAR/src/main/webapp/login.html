Welcome

This is the network server for the computer assisted participatory modeling project at El Colegio de La Frontera Sur (ECOSUR) in San Cristobal de Las Casas, funded by CONACYT. We have placed computer games developed here as a result of our ongoing research in the Tablon watershed at the La Sepultura UNESCO world heritage site.

Our computer games implement board games created by researchers and stakeholders in La Sepultura. These games have been adapted and/or extended for online play. We follow the COMMOD process, with our results being based primarily on the gaming aspects of participatory modeling but also making use of multi-agent modeling for experimentation and artificial intelligence.

GAMES

Currently, we host two rule based games, GENTE and MANANTIALES, with our rules based platform.

Gente is a fairly simple board game, adapted from "PENTE" and played on a go-type board. The work was developed to explore self-centered versus selfless behaviors in the context of a four player, team based, game. Players compete as a team and against each other by striving to create 2 sets of three aligned tokens of the same color (individual competition) or 3 sets of four aligned tokens whch mix team colors.

Manantiales is more complicated than Gente, and is the amalgamation of several earlier model games and discussions begun at ECOSUR during the ARIDNet workshop in July of 2008. Although Gente has been played with stakeholders across the spectrum, the abstraction of the game made it difficult for stakeholders to help us completely extend and adapt the game from our initial design. Work on interactive game rules is progressing to try and address this challenge from the computer assisted perspective. In the case of Manantiales, we have had very active stakeholder participation and co-development starting with the AridNET workshop and working through many sessions and workshops in the field, government offices in Tuxtla Gutierrez and in the academic environment here at ECOSUR.

METHODOLOGY

As we mention above, our games have been developed following the COMMOD process, first articulated by F. Bousquet and Guy Trebuil (et. al.) in the seminal paper, "An environmental modeling approach: the use of multi-agent simulations." We have focused our work on iterative game designs with a structure faithful to environmental models while using Game Theory to qualify outcomes and AI participation. In the case of Manantiales, modeling works to express a stylized simulation of what is actually occurring through development in the Tablon watershed/Sepultura buffer zone.

ARCHITECTURE

The diagram that follows is a simplified view of our JEE rules based architecture for games. We use a distributed architecture, allowing our games to be edited, modified and run on a variety of client platforms and our server software to scale to any size. Our current iteration includes a Flash based gaming client; but clients can be developed and written in a variety of technologies that allow for RPC, SOAP or REST based messaging to signal our backend implementation. We are currently thinking about implementations for smaller handheld devices (Android, XHTML, WML) as well as Apple iPad/iPhone devices. We are very interested in collaborating with software developers who have an interest in our project and any of these platforms.

As I mention above, the diagram only roughly sketches some of the structure of our backend implementation. A common model, described by a set of interfaces and serializable container classes (the Bridge Pattern) interact with a set of common EJBs capable of being externalized via REST or SOAP protocols. We use the Drools rules engine to govern interactions in the game as well as the AI implementation (a fully runnable multi-agent system when considered from a COMMOD perspective). Agents act through rules triggered by modifications to the underlying set of entity beans which act as an implementation of the common model on a per game basis. Agents and the game itself communicate change by means of a JMS Topic queue; which notifies Agent implementations attached to the JMS message bus by means of MessageDrivenBeans and BLAZEDS signaling to the Flash user interface.
